Since the 1990s, there has been a growing international interest in Jewish studies. Among the Jewish communities incorporated into the modern Greek state during the 19th and 20th centuries, Thessaloniki—once a prominent Jewish center of the Ottoman Empire—has drawn the most scholarly attention. In recent years, several smaller Jewish communities such as those of Athens, Rhodes, and Kavala have also begun to be explored more systematically. Yet the Jewish history of Crete remains largely overlooked in contemporary research. While the sinking of the steamship Tanais and the destruction of the island’s Jewish population in June 1944 are well-known, our understanding of the life and history of Cretan Jews before the war remains fragmentary.
View of the Jewish neighbourhood in Hania, early 20th century © Jewish Museum of Greece
The research project "The Jews of Crete: Reconstructing an Absence, Narrating a Presence" seeks to fill this gap. Drawing on written, oral and visual sources, it examines the life of the Jewish community of Hania—the largest Jewish community in Crete during the 19th and 20th centuries—during the turbulent years leading up to World War II. The project also examines how the memory and legacy of Hania’s Jews have been managed after their physical destruction. Beyond studying a single community, the project also aims to contribute to a broader discussion on the function, character, and networks of small Jewish communities across Greece
The research was carried out with the support of the Etz Hayyim Synagogue of Hania and Anne Germanacos.
